Research & Motivation

With respect to astrophysics and cosmology, we live in truly fascinating times. Upcoming surveys will allow us to map a large part of our universe, looking far back in time, with unprecedented precision. We will have the opportunity to learn more about the universe than ever before, which will help us to address long-standing questions in cosmology: What is the nature of dark energy, this mysterious component causing the accelerated expansion of space? How well does Einstein's theory of general relativity actually work? Is it the final answer to all our questions, or can we find the imprints of previously unknown physics?

As we will gain an enormous amount of high-precision cosmological data in the next two decades, we have a good chance to solve these mysteries – or at least come a great step closer to doing so! But this optimism should come with caution, as these advances in measurements also lead to more room for potentially wrong conclusions. As we are mapping out large parts of our universe with high precision, we are entering a regime where not all observables are described well by linear Newtonian physics. In my research, I focus on developing precise theoretical formalisms for cosmological observables such as the galaxy number density, the distortion in the luminosity distance, and cosmic shear. In particular, I account for general relativistic effects in these formalisms, and investigate their impact on observables numerically.

To conclude, my research goal is to contribute to our ability to draw the correct conclusions from upcoming surveys, in particular when considering large-scale data. This way, maybe we can indeed be optimistic and hope to find some correct answers to the mysteries of the universe!
